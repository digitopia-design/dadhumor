The Anatomy Of A Dad Joke.

We've analysed 50,000 user reactions across our entire joke catalogue. Here's the structural DNA of the perfect setup-and-punchline combo - and the formula that makes you groan against your will.

A dad joke is not just any bad joke. It's a specific, finely-tuned comedic mechanism that has evolved over decades of after-dinner deployment. Strip away the affection and the cringe, and you're left with a remarkably consistent structure.

We define a dad joke by three properties: brevity, predictability, and groan-factor. Get all three right and you've got a punchline that hits like a thrown slipper. Get one wrong and it's just a joke. Get two wrong and you're at an open mic night.

The setup-punchline ratio

The platonic dad joke is short. Of the top 100 most-Propped jokes on this site, 87% have setups under 12 words. The setup builds expectation; the punchline detonates it. Drag the setup out and you lose the rhythm. Consider:

EMBEDDED JOKE
Why don't eggs tell jokes?
They'd crack each other up.

The setup is six words. The punchline is six words. That's not coincidence - it's structural balance. The punchline mirrors the setup's length but pivots its meaning. It's almost mathematical.

📊 The Data
Our most-Propped joke ("They'd crack each other up.") has a setup-to-punchline word ratio of 1:1. The 10 next-best jokes average 1.2:1. Anything beyond 1.6:1 sees engagement drop by 40%.

The pun is everything

Of our top 50 jokes by Props, 78% are pun-based. The remaining 22% are roughly split between wordplay and observational humour. Anti-humour - the deliberately disappointing punchline - underperforms on Props but over-performs on Groans, which is its own kind of victory.

The single highest-engagement joke on the platform isn't even one of the funniest. It's the one that earns the most violent eye-roll. That's the secret of the dad joke as a category: groans count as engagement.

💡 Tip
When writing your own dad jokes, prioritise the pun. If the punchline isn't a wordplay, it's not really a dad joke - it's just a short joke. There's a difference, and your audience will subconsciously feel it.

Why this hybrid works

  • ✓ Brand identity stays untouched. The joke app, share cards, mascot moments - all stay on the dark, cyber-yellow visual language people will associate with Dad Humor.
  • ✓ Articles get the right reading experience. Long-form prose IS easier on cream than midnight. Inter on cream at 17px / 1.75 line-height is genuinely comfortable.
  • ✓ Yellow still pops in light mode. The "yellow inside black pill" treatment becomes the brand's signature highlight - a visual hat tip to the dark app, used consistently for: punchlines, key takeaways, headline highlights, embedded jokes.
  • ✓ Embedded jokes use a dark card on light pages. When you embed a joke into an article, it appears in a midnight-coloured box - a small window into the app's visual world. Reinforces brand without disrupting reading flow.
  • ✓ Toggle only on articles, only optional. Users who want dark for articles can switch, but light is default. No theme toggle in the joke app at all.
  • ✓ Accessibility wins on both fronts. Dark app contrast is 17:1 on punchlines. Light articles contrast is 16:1 on body text. Everything passes WCAG AAA.

Things this approach decides for you

  • ✓ Stache mascot: Keep PNG assets as-is (designed for dark). When Stache appears in articles, he's inside the embedded-joke dark cards. Problem solved.
  • ✓ Share cards: Always dark. No light variants needed. Father's Day, OG images, signage - all stay consistent.
  • ✓ Email lead magnet PDF: Could go either way. Recommend dark to feel like the brand artefact users expect.
  • ✓ WP plugin: Default dark, allow site owners to override to light. Best of both for distribution.
  • ✗ Skipping: Full dual theme system on the joke app (would dilute the brand)
